HOW TO CHOOSE THE RIGHT FIXED DEPOSIT — A PRACTICAL GUIDE

STEP 1: SAFETY FIRST — KNOW YOUR BANK TYPE
Not all FDs are equal in safety. From safest to least safe:

1. Government-backed: Post Office Time Deposits, RBI Bonds — backed by Government of India directly. Zero default risk.
2. Large public sector banks (SBI, Bank of Baroda): Implicitly government-backed, very low risk. DICGC insured.
3. Large private banks (HDFC, ICICI, Axis): Well-regulated, strong balance sheets, DICGC insured.
4. Small finance banks (AU, Ujjivan, Jana, Suryoday): RBI-licensed, DICGC insured, slightly higher risk than large banks — compensated by 0.5-1.5% higher rates. For amounts under ₹5 lakh, excellent risk-reward balance.
5. NBFCs (Shriram, Mahindra Finance): NOT DICGC insured. Higher rates (8.5-10%) but carry credit risk. Only invest if NBFC is rated AAA or AA+ by CRISIL/ICRA, and limit to 10-15% of savings.
---
STEP 2: INTEREST RATE — COMPARE AFTER-TAX RETURNS
Higher rate is better, but always calculate post-tax returns based on your tax slab:

For someone in 0% tax slab: Full interest is yours. 9% is genuinely 9%.
For someone in 5% slab: 9% FD gives ~8.55% post-tax.
For someone in 20% slab: 9% FD gives ~7.2% post-tax.
For someone in 30% slab: 9% FD gives ~6.3% post-tax.

If you're in the 20-30% tax slab, also consider tax-free alternatives like PPF (7.1%, tax-free) or RBI Floating Rate Bonds (8.05%, taxable but can be compared).

Always compare rates from multiple banks on the same day — rates change frequently and what's on a bank's website may be outdated.
---
STEP 3: TENOR — MATCH TO YOUR FINANCIAL GOAL
The single most important factor is matching tenor to your financial need:

Short-term goals (under 12 months): Use short-duration FDs (3, 6, or 9 months). Small finance banks offer good rates even for short tenors.

Medium-term goals (1-2 years): This is the sweet spot — most banks offer their best rates for 12-24 month tenors. Good for goals like home renovation, buying a vehicle, or children's school fees.

Long-term goals (3-5 years): Use 3-year or 5-year FDs. Consider whether you might need the money early — premature withdrawal penalties can be painful. Post Office 5-year TD qualifies for Section 80C deduction.

Never lock money you might need in an emergency in an FD — keep 3-6 months of expenses in a savings account or liquid fund.
---
STEP 4: LIQUIDITY — CAN YOU ACCESS MONEY IF NEEDED?
Before investing, ask yourself: What if I need this money suddenly?

Check premature withdrawal terms: Most banks allow it with 0.5-1% penalty. Some NBFCs restrict it.
Laddering strategy (highly recommended): Instead of putting ₹1,00,000 in one FD for 1 year, split it into:
- ₹33,000 in a 4-month FD
- ₹33,000 in a 8-month FD  
- ₹34,000 in a 12-month FD
This way, every few months, some FD matures and you have access to money without breaking anything early. You also reinvest at whatever rates are current — benefiting if rates rise.
---
STEP 5: COMPARING TWO FDs — QUICK FRAMEWORK
Ask these 5 questions:
1. Is the bank DICGC-insured? (If no and it's an NBFC, check credit rating)
2. What is the effective post-tax rate given my slab?
3. Does the tenor match my goal?
4. What is the premature withdrawal penalty?
5. Is ₹5 lakh DICGC limit being breached at this bank?

Example comparison:
Unity Small Finance Bank: 9% p.a., DICGC insured, 1% penalty for early exit — good for amount under ₹5 lakh.
Shriram Finance: 8.85% p.a., NOT DICGC insured, 2% penalty — suitable for investors comfortable with credit risk.
SBI: 6.8% p.a., DICGC insured, extremely safe — choose if safety is paramount and rate doesn't matter.
